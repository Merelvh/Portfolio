Affiliate website design

About the Project

The client asked us to make a design for their new affiliate website BestDayDeals which required a:

  • home page that would display all products without categories,
  • product page which should consist of product information and highlights, volume discount options, payment method and a countdown timer of 30 minutes. Users would only land on this website through another one by clicking on a specific productlink.

My role

My role as an intern at Activate Your Business in this project was to define the specific needs and goals, creating wireframes and being responsible for most of the ultimate design. In this project I was the main designer, receiving feedback from my supervisor.

The goal

The goal of the website is therefore a quick and easy checkout process as the customer already knows which product they would like to purchase.

The goal of the client is to let the customer check out as soon as possible and for them to not leave the page in the meantime. To reach this goal, the customer must have the opportunity to check out on the page as where the product is shown. The customer needs as few steps as possible to achieve this goal.

Research

My research mainly focused on the product page as this is the first page customers will land on when clicking on a product link.

To understand which elements should be present in the design, it was necessary to empathize with both the client and the customer and keeping the goals in consideration.

I started with gathering information about affiliate websites. My research consisted of analyzing multiple similar websites where customers land by clicking on an affiliate link.

I also searched for websites that utilize one-page checkout principles to understand what is necessary for an easy and fast checkout process. Examples that fit this criterea are Dribbble and Behance.

We want to guide the customer in a certain direction using persuasive design, however with an ethical point of view. This means that it needs to provides a reasonable benefit to both the client and the customer.

In my design I considered following five principles of persuasive design.

  1. Clarity above all

    2. For the customers it’s important to quickly see what kind of site it is, what they can do there and what could be expected from them.

    Therefore, the affiliate website should make it easy for them to find sufficient product information and to make it clear that they can buy the product on the same page. This also means that the entire check out procedure is represented on the same page. This way we provide clarity and transparency.

  2. Visual appeal

    3. On the affiliate website, the first impression is the most important one, because it simply doesn’t get another chance. The customer just lands on the product page and can either buy the product or leave the site.

  3. Strong visual hierarchy

    4. It is important to show the product information with the most significance. This would be by followed by the checkout procedure.

    Considering visual hierarchy, I divided the colors into four commonly used ecommerce color groups: Call to action, Urgence Association, Positive Association, Trusted Assocation.

  4. Conserve attention

    5. To get the customers attention, I wanted to use popping colors and provide the most important information above the fold, as this is where 80% of user attention takes place.

  5. One primary action per screen

    6. The primary action for the affiliate website would be buying the product. We don’t want to distract the customer with related products, links to categories or other information.

Wireframing

After gathering enough examples and inspiration, I started creating Lo-Fi wireframes. I used the wireframes to define the allocation of elements on the pages and to receive feedback from the client without them getting distracted by the overall look and feel of the UI.

I created multiple wireframes with different layouts to be able to start a brainstorm session about the best implementation, for both mobile and desktop.

At first, we wanted the checkout procedure to be above the first fold together with the product information. By creating an accordion type of checkout procedure, all the steps are directly visible which provides clarity for the customer in completing the process.

Instead of the checkout process being above the first fold, they would like to fully use that space for the product information. To meet their expectations, but also deliver a good user experience and and achieve the goals, I made some informed changes to the design.

Design

To create urgency, a sticky countdown timer is added on the top of the screen with a prominent, attention-getting color. Below the product information, another element of urgency is placed which is still visible when the customer scrolls down the page.

All product information is clearly presented above the first fold. Reviews about the product can be seen at the bottom of the page by clicking on the number of reviews.

Above the first fold, a CTA button is placed in a prominent place to guide the customer to the checkout process below, because the checkout procedure isn’t visible above the first fold. The checkout process is fully “unfolded” to show all the steps to the customer and creating an even more transparent process.

By default, the highest discount option in the volume discount section is selected. This is being made clear by clear use of colors, discount labels and a checkmark.

The input fields offer floating labels so the customer had the possibility to see which field they’re filling in.

At the shipping details section, the switch for delivery address is the same as details is selected by default, same for standard delivery.

Handoff

After completing the design, I saved one version for the developers including the assets for colors and elements and different statuses of the components.

A key point this project taught me was to adapt and respond with changes that are being made during the design process while also maintaining the original goals of the client.
